Mountains, valleys, rivers, and hills are not just characteristics of a landscape - they are the landscape. Each is reliant on the other. Mountains influence rivers, valleys affect vegetation, and all aspects of the terrain combine to create a whole that is greater than the sum of its parts. That’s not just gestalt - it’s geography!

Everything is related to everything else, and near things are more related than distant things.

â€” Waldo Tobler’s First Law of Geography

Yet too often, fantasy maps are created as if the individual features of a landscape can be bolted on separate from everything else.

This tutorial will show you how to apply realistic (and beautiful!) terrain cartography to fantasy maps in a way that is congruent with geographic principles.-READMORE-

This tutorial was designed for Photoshop, but all the techniques shown are commonly supported in other tools (e.g., GIMP). Feel free to use any tools that you’re comfortable with.

Step 1: Document Set-up and Land Creation

The first step in creating a fantasy map is drawing the basic shape of the land. There are a number of ways to do this, from freely sketching the shape as you desire to tracing an existing map. To get up and running quickly, we’ll use a technique that generates a realistic body of land in seconds: tracing a cloud.

If you already have the basic shape of your world or continenet drawn out, you can skip this step and jump ahead to Step 2.

Step 1a: Create a new document in Photoshop

In Photoshop, create a new document. I’ve chosen a size that maintains a 16:9 ratio, but feel free to adjust the height and width as you like.

A good landmass shouldn’t have too many smooth edges or unnatural corners. The coast should be sharp and jagged, with plenty of bays, imperfections, and variation. Clouds provide a great shortcut to generating land areas with these traits.

Step 1b: Use a cloud to generate a land mass

Simply searching for images of clouds led to the following image:

We want to bring this into our document. Right-click the cloud image and select ‘Copy Image.’ Switch back to your Photoshop document and paste the image (Ctrl-v or Cmd-v).

Use the Magic Wand tool to select the sky around the cloud. This selects everything except the cloud we want. To swap the selection to get only the cloud with the sky, press Ctrl+i or Cmd+i to invert the selection.

Create a new layer and simply fill the selected area with grey.

Great. Now we have a nice body of land to work with.

Step 2: Adding Rivers and Lakes

When adding rivers, take advantage of the imperfections along the coast. Try to find areas that naturally cut inland and look like they’ve been eroded by moving water. Places and drawing lakes is a little easier, and you can mostly use your imagination. But do keep scale in mind. If you invasion your continent to be 2,500 kilometers across, a lake that would be 750km long wouldn’t be too realistic. Draw as few or many lakes as you wish; none would be OK, too! It’s your world, you decide what it looks like.

Drawing lakes is easy. Simply use the pen tool in shape mode. While using short, curved lines, draw the outline of a lake. Try to make the lakes look a little irregular, without too round or too angular. It’s OK if their shorelines are unnaturally smooth. This will be fixed later.

Rivers can be made using a combination of the pen tool and brushes.

  • Select the pen tool and make sure it is in path mode.
  • Start your river just off the coast, where the sea would be. Click and make small, curved lines.
  • Continue drawing the path for the river in a serpentine fashion. Make an oxbow or two, if you like.
  • Once your path is done, select the brush panel and change the settings to be similar to those below:
  • With the brush defined, make sure the pen tool is activated again and right-click the path. Select Stroke with brush. This will apply the brush to your path, ensuring it is wide at the river’s mouth and tapers throughout its length.
  • Repeat this process to draw several rivers. Maybe some even lead to your lake(s). Alter the brush settings to give your rivers variation in their widths.

Rivers in nature are formed by moving water, not the pen tool. Accordingly, real rivers and other inland waterbodies are not smooth and perfect like the ones our brush has made. We will correct this in the next step.
