Chances are you’ve heard about John Snow’s 1854 map of cholera outbreaks in London. If you’re a geographer, cartographer, GIS professional, or student studying anything geospatial…chances are you’ve heard about Snow’s map a lot.

This map is considred a classic example of spatial anlysis by universities and businesses alike. It is showcased as a precursor to GIS and frequently lauded as a landmark example of geospatial problem solving. More than just a map, Snow’s plotting of cholera cases is a cornerstone in the histories of geography, social science, and epidiomology.

Unfortunately, much of that history is plain wrong.

What follows is a brief critique of the narrative surrounding Snow’s map. and a discussion on what it means to learn about spatial analysis in 2015.

Map and Myth

The story as most people know it

John Snow, a physician in London, maticuously mapped every cholera case in London. He noticed a propensity of outbreaks near wellsâ€”especially a popular well on Broad Street. He urged officials to close the well - and just like that - cholera outbreaks ceased.

This confirmed Snow’s theory on water-borne cholera, gave Snow the title ‘father of epidemiology,’ and placed the map in history books.

Unfortunately, most of that just isn’t true.

Broad Street? More like Fraud Street, am I right?!

What actually happened

John Snow was a physician who had been studying cholera for a long time. It was his life’s work. Snow, an oppontent of the myiasma theory (in which yada yada), wanted to prove that (a) cholera spread by biological and natural means, and (b) science could identify these means through experimentation.

To do that, Snow hypothesized that the source of cholera is likely something familiar to those who caught the disease. He gathered no shortage of personal belongings, food, and even the drinking water from the afflicted. But he didn’t map any of this.

Instead, Snow put it under the microscope.

In 1849, Snow publushed his findings in an essay On the mode of transmission of Cholera. The date of this essay is of great significance: it comes five years earlier than the map. In other words: John Snow hypothesized, and proved, how cholera spread long before the map was ever produced.

Moreover, geographic relationships and spatial analysis played no part in the creation or conclusion of Snow’s theory.
